Any object in motion has kinetic energy, but what actually determines how much of it an object has? Take a kicked soccer ball flying toward the goal. As long as it's moving, it has this energy. Think of kinetic energy as the literal oomph behind the ball, dictating exactly how hard it will hit the target. It's tempting to think this oomph comes entirely from speed. Watch this light bicycle pedaling incredibly fast. Its meter shoots up. But speed is only half the story. Nast matters just as much. Look at this heavy delivery truck rolling slowly next to the bike. Because the truck is so massive, its meter spikes way past the bikes. Proving that a heavy, slow object can actually pack more kinetic energy than a light-fast one, which brings us right back to our flying soccer ball. When it finally slams into the net, that impact is the pure result of its combined mass and speed.
